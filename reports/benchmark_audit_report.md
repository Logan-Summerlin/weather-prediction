# Benchmark Audit Report — Critical Analysis

**Date:** 2026-02-10
**Scope:** Deep audit of `prediction_market_benchmark_report.md`, `full_benchmark_report.md`, and all supporting code/data.

---

## Executive Summary

The benchmark reports contain **multiple critical issues** that undermine the validity of the analysis and the conclusions drawn. The single most damaging problem: **the benchmark tests the wrong model**. The model being benchmarked (vanilla [128,64] NN, MAE 4.44°F) is an obsolete pipeline that performs 2.2x worse than the current best model (Phase 1 5-seed ensemble, MAE 1.987°F). Every conclusion about "our model vs. the market" is therefore moot — we are benchmarking a model we already know is bad.

Beyond the wrong-model problem, the audit identifies 12 additional issues spanning statistical methodology, trading simulation mechanics, calibration interpretation, and report accuracy.

---

## CRITICAL Issues (Invalidate Conclusions)

### 1. Benchmark Tests the Wrong Model (Severity: FATAL)

**Finding:** The benchmark uses predictions from `data/max_train_nn_predictions_is.csv` and `_oos.csv`, generated by `scripts/generate_max_training_predictions.py`. This script trains a vanilla `TempPredictorV1([128, 64])` with 50 features (47 station lag-1 TMAX + NYC AR + sin/cos day-of-year), Huber loss, no MOS integration.

**Verified MAE:**
- IS (2023-2024): 4.44°F
- OOS (2025): 4.53°F

**Current best model** (Phase 1 improvement, per `reports/phase1_improvement_report.md`):
- Test MAE: **1.987°F** (5-seed ensemble)
- OOS MAE: **2.018°F**

The best model uses MOS error memory, MOS×station interactions, temporal features (day length, solar elevation, anomaly), spatial features (gradients, frontal proxy), and a 5-seed ensemble. **None of these appear in the benchmarked model.**

**Impact:** The report concludes "Our NN model underperforms both benchmarks" and "NWS is the better trading signal, suggesting our model's value-add beyond public forecasts is currently negative." These conclusions are **meaningless** because they describe a model 2.2x worse than what we actually have. The best model (MAE 2.02°F) is within striking distance of NWS (MAE 2.23°F) and might outperform it.

### 2. Report Mischaracterizes Sigma as "6.3°F Constant" (Severity: HIGH)

Sigma ranges from **3.87°F to 7.87°F** — a 2x factor. The sigma is constant *within* each month but varies substantially across months. The report's characterization is inaccurate.

However, even the monthly sigma is crude. March sigma of 7.87°F means the model's 95% CI spans ~31°F, which makes it nearly impossible to assign meaningful probability to any 2°F Kalshi bucket.

### 3. Catastrophic Calibration Failure Masked by ECE Metric (Severity: HIGH)

The reliability diagram data reveals the model's probability estimates are not just poorly calibrated — they are **inversely correlated** with reality above the 0.25 bin:

| Predicted Prob | Actual Occurrence Rate | Interpretation |
|---------------|----------------------|----------------|
| 0.05 | 16.2% | Under-predicts by 3x |
| 0.15 | 20.9% | Under-predicts by 1.4x |
| 0.25 | 11.2% | Over-predicts by 2.2x |
| 0.35 | 12.3% | Over-predicts by 2.8x |
| 0.45 | 7.9% | Over-predicts by 5.7x |
| 0.55 | 8.9% | Over-predicts by 6.2x |
| 0.65 | 5.4% | Over-predicts by **12x** |
| 0.95 | 41.4% | Over-predicts by 2.3x |

This is pathological. When the model says "65% likely," the event occurs 5.4% of the time. The ECE of 0.14 does not convey the severity — this model's probability outputs are essentially noise above p=0.25. An ECE-weighted summary hides this because most observations fall in the low-probability bins where the error is smaller in absolute terms.

## MAJOR Issues (Affect Validity of Trading Analysis)

### 4. Trading Simulation Ignores Bid-Ask Spread (Severity: HIGH)

The trading sim in `test_model_vs_benchmarks.py:run_trading_sim()` uses `presettlement_prob` (bid/ask midpoint) as the execution price. Real Kalshi trading requires crossing the spread:

- **YES trade cost** = ask price (higher than mid)
- **NO trade cost** = 1 - NO bid (higher than 1 - mid)

From the pre-settlement data, bid-ask spreads are visible (e.g., bid=11 cents, ask=84 cents — a 73-cent spread). Even typical liquid contracts have 1-5 cent spreads. The simulation systematically **overstates profitability** by ignoring this cost.

**Estimated impact:** For the NWS strategy at 0.20 threshold (1,255 trades, $174 P&L), even a 2-cent average spread cost reduces P&L by ~$25 (14% of total). Wider spreads on illiquid contracts would be worse.

### 5. Sharpe Ratio Inconsistency Across Codebase (Severity: HIGH)

Two different Sharpe calculations exist:

- `test_model_vs_benchmarks.py:398` — **Non-annualized**: `mean(P&L) / std(P&L)`
- `src/trading.py:827` — **Annualized**: `(mean(P&L) / std(P&L)) * sqrt(252)`

The benchmark report shows Sharpe ratios of 0.15-0.35, calculated using the non-annualized version. If annualized (which is standard practice for comparing strategies), these would be 2.4x-5.6x higher. The report doesn't specify which convention is used, making the numbers uninterpretable without reading the code.

### 6. OOS Sample Size is Statistically Insufficient (Severity: HIGH)

The OOS period (2025) contains only **215 bucket observations across ~36 dates**. For the trading simulation:

| Strategy | OOS Trades | OOS P&L | Statistical Power |
|----------|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| Model @ 0.20 | 80 | $4.07 | Meaningless |
| NWS @ 0.20 | 59 | $3.34 | Meaningless |
| NWS @ 0.15 | 76 | $3.85 | Meaningless |

With 59-80 trades, the standard error of the mean P&L is enormous. A Sharpe ratio calculated from 59 trades has a confidence interval that easily spans from negative to positive. The report presents OOS trading results without any confidence intervals or statistical significance tests. The statement "Both remain profitable in OOS (2025)" is not supported by the evidence.

### 7. Flat Position Sizing (1 Contract Per Signal) (Severity: MEDIUM)

The simulation trades exactly 1 contract per signal regardless of edge magnitude. Real trading requires position sizing:

- **Kelly criterion** (implemented in `src/trading.py` but not used in the benchmark)
- **Risk management** (max loss per trade, daily limits)
- **Liquidity constraints** (can't trade 100 contracts on a market with 10 contracts of open interest)

The P&L numbers ($115-$182 over 2 years) are for 1-contract trades. Scaling up introduces liquidity impact. The ROI percentages (14-33%) are technically correct for the capital at risk but assume unlimited market depth.

## MODERATE Issues (Affect Interpretation)

### 8. Two Reports Show Different Brier Scores for the Same Sources (Severity: MEDIUM)

`benchmark_report.md` (from `build_nws_benchmark.py`):
- 6,542 bucket-rows across 1,095 dates
- NWS Brier: 0.1368, Model Brier: 0.1780

`full_benchmark_report.md` (from `test_model_vs_benchmarks.py`):
- 4,261 bucket-rows across 765 dates
- NWS Brier: 0.1407, Model Brier: 0.1793

The discrepancy exists because `full_benchmark_report.md` only includes dates where pre-settlement Kalshi data exists (inner join with pre-settlement data), while `benchmark_report.md` uses all dates with settled Kalshi data. The PM report (`prediction_market_benchmark_report.md`) cherry-picks numbers from both reports without noting they are computed on different sample sets. The NWS Brier score jumps from 0.1368 to 0.1407 depending on which subset of dates is used — a shift comparable to the NWS-Model gap itself.

### 9. NWS "Bias Correction" Creates Information Leakage Risk (Severity: MEDIUM)

The NWS benchmark applies monthly bias correction: `nws_mu = MOS_forecast + bias[month]`, where bias is computed on pre-2023 training data. While the temporal boundary is clean (no future data leak), there is a subtle issue: **the bias correction assumes stationarity of MOS bias over time**. If NWS improves their MOS system (which they periodically do), the historical bias pattern may not generalize forward.

The reported NWS MAE:
- Raw MOS: 2.33°F
- Bias-corrected: 2.23°F (improvement of 0.10°F)

This 0.10°F improvement from bias correction is modest and probably legitimate, but it should be noted that a real-time trader would not have perfect monthly bias estimates — they'd be noisy rolling estimates.

### 10. Pre-Settlement Snapshot Timing Favors the Market (Severity: MEDIUM)

The pre-settlement snapshots are from the evening/night of day t-1 (56.5% at midnight ET, 29.1% at 10-11 PM ET). At this time, Kalshi market participants have access to:

- Evening NWS forecasts (including MOS)
- GFS/NAM/ECMWF model outputs from 00Z and 12Z runs
- Afternoon observations from day t-1
- Any local knowledge or ensemble model outputs

Our NN model uses only: lag-1 station TMAX + NYC lag-1 + sin/cos day encoding. **The market has strictly more information.** Comparing these is not a fair apples-to-apples forecast competition — it's comparing a pure-station-observation model against the aggregated wisdom of weather-informed traders. The proper comparison would be our model *plus* MOS features vs. the market.

### 11. Bucket Probability Sums Not Validated (Severity: LOW)

Neither `test_model_vs_benchmarks.py` nor `build_nws_benchmark.py` validates that model/NWS bucket probabilities sum to 1.0 for a given date. With the Gaussian CDF approach, this should hold approximately if buckets span the full support, but:

- The "below" and "above" buckets have finite thresholds
- If bucket structure doesn't cover the full range, probabilities won't sum to 1
- This could systematically bias Brier scores if missing probability mass correlates with outcomes

### 12. Market Probability Extraction May Be Inaccurate (Severity: LOW)

`src/kalshi_client.py:get_market_implied_probability()` uses only bid prices from the orderbook to compute implied probabilities. Standard practice is to use the bid-ask midpoint from the full orderbook. Using bids alone systematically underestimates implied probability for YES contracts and overestimates for NO contracts.

## Factual Errors in the PM Report

1. **"sigma (6.3°F constant)"** — Sigma varies from 3.87 to 7.87 by month; not constant.
2. **"MAE 4.48°F"** — Actual IS MAE is 4.44°F (minor rounding issue, but the report cites three different numbers: 4.48 overall, 4.45 IS, 4.53 OOS. The report's "overall" 4.48 is not the average of the IS and OOS numbers — it's unclear what period it covers).
3. **"Our NN model underperforms both benchmarks"** — True for this model, but irrelevant because this is not our best model. The Phase 1 improved model (MAE 2.02°F) was never benchmarked.

## Recommendations

### Immediate (Before Drawing Any Conclusions)

1. **Re-run the entire benchmark with the Phase 1 improved model.** Generate new prediction files using the best available model (5-seed ensemble with MOS features, error memory, spatial/temporal features). The current benchmark is testing an obsolete model and all conclusions are invalid for the current state of the art.

2. **Add bid-ask spread modeling to the trading simulation.** At minimum, add a configurable spread cost per contract (e.g., 2-5 cents). Better: use the actual bid/ask from the pre-settlement data.

3. **Add confidence intervals to OOS results.** Bootstrap the trading P&L to get 95% CIs. If the CI includes zero, the OOS profitability claim is unsupported.

### Short-Term (Improve Benchmark Quality)

4. **Standardize Sharpe ratio calculation** across the codebase. Use annualized Sharpe consistently and document the convention.

5. **Validate bucket probability sums** for each date. Flag any date where model or NWS probabilities don't sum to [0.95, 1.05].

6. **Use per-sample sigma** instead of monthly constants. The Phase 1 probabilistic model (D_Probabilistic) produces per-sample sigma with calibrated 95% PIs — this should be used for the benchmark.

7. **Report ECE alongside full reliability diagrams** in the PM report. The ECE alone hides the severity of the calibration failure.

### Strategic (For Market Competitiveness)

8. **The NWS comparison is the right first target.** Before trying to beat the Kalshi market, our model must first match or beat NWS (MAE 2.23°F). With Phase 1 at 2.02°F, we may already be there. Re-benchmark to confirm.

9. **MOS integration is table stakes.** The market already incorporates MOS. Any model that doesn't include MOS as a feature is at an information disadvantage by construction.

10. **Focus on Spring (MAM).** The Phase 1 model shows OOS MAE of 2.85°F in spring vs. 1.63°F in summer. Spring is where the most edge can be gained — and likely where the market is least efficient.

---

## Conclusion

The benchmark analysis infrastructure is well-built — the code is clean, the scoring metrics are correctly implemented, and the data pipeline is properly constructed with no temporal leakage. The fundamental problem is that it was pointed at the wrong model. The entire trading analysis, Brier score comparison, and calibration discussion applies to a vanilla 50-feature NN that we already know underperforms. Before any conclusions about market competitiveness can be drawn, the benchmark must be re-run with the current best model.
