## 4. Gap Analysis

### 4.1 Major Directional Hole: SSE (132 - 194 deg)

The largest angular gap in the network spans **62.2 degrees** between JFK (bearing 131.9 deg) and Atlantic City Marina (bearing 194.1 deg). This SSE corridor corresponds to the direction from NYC across lower New York Harbor, the New Jersey coast, and out over the Atlantic Ocean.

**Why this gap exists:** From Central Park, the SSE direction (roughly 135-195 deg) crosses Jamaica Bay, the Rockaways, Sandy Hook, and then the open Atlantic. There is essentially no habitable land mass with weather stations in this angular range beyond about 20 miles. The New Jersey shoreline curves westward, so coastal NJ stations (Atlantic City) register as S/SSW rather than SE.

**Is this a real problem?** Partially. The SSE direction is dominated by the Atlantic Ocean, so the absence of stations here is a geographic inevitability rather than a network design flaw. JFK (14mi, 132 deg) and the two Atlantic City stations (~97-100mi, 194-199 deg) bracket this gap from either side. The ocean moderating effect from this direction is partially captured by the E sector stations on Long Island's south shore and the S sector Atlantic City stations. However, onshore flow events from the SSE (e.g., coastal nor'easters approaching from the south) may be poorly anticipated.

### 4.2 Secondary Gap: ESE (96 - 132 deg)

A **36-degree** gap exists between Farmingdale (bearing 95.9 deg, E sector) and JFK (bearing 131.9 deg, SE sector). This is the transition zone between Long Island's north shore (E sector) and the SE direction. While below the 60-degree threshold, it represents the only land-based gap that could potentially be filled.

**Potential fill stations:** A station on Long Island's south shore near Babylon, Lindenhurst, or Long Beach (roughly 15-25mi at bearing ~110-125 deg) would bridge this gap. GHCN stations in this area are scarce -- most Long Island south shore reporting stations are part of the NWS cooperative network rather than first-order airports.

### 4.3 SE Sector Deep Dive

The SE sector (112.5-157.5 deg) contains only **JFK at 14.5 miles**. This is the network's most significant coverage deficiency by any measure:

- **Ring coverage:** Ring1 only. No Ring2, Ring3, or Ring4 stations.
- **Angular coverage:** JFK sits at 131.9 deg, covering only the center of the 45-degree sector.
- **Meteorological impact:** The SE direction is important for detecting Atlantic moisture advection and coastal storm approach. However, the signal from this direction is partially captured by adjacent sectors:
  - E sector stations on eastern Long Island (Montauk, Westhampton) capture Atlantic influence from the east.
  - S sector stations (Atlantic City) capture coastal influence from the south.

**Can this gap be practically filled?** No, not significantly. Beyond JFK in the SE direction lies Jamaica Bay, the Rockaway Peninsula, and then open ocean. There are no GHCN-quality stations on the barrier islands or offshore. The closest possibility would be Floyd Bennett Field (a historical station at ~bearing 145 deg, ~12mi), but it has limited modern data availability. The gap from 14.5mi to open ocean is an irreducible geographic constraint.

### 4.4 South Sector Near-Field Gap

The S sector (157.5-202.5 deg) has no station closer than **97 miles** (Atlantic City). This means temperature signals approaching from directly south take roughly 6-12 hours to traverse the gap before reaching the nearest S-sector observation point. For the day-ahead prediction task (t-1 to t), this is acceptable but not ideal.

**Potential fill stations:** The GHCN network includes stations in central New Jersey that could help:
- **Lakehurst NAS / Joint Base McGuire-Dix-Lakehurst, NJ** (~50mi, bearing ~195 deg) -- a military installation with historical GHCN data (station ID: USW00014780 or similar). Would provide a Ring1/Ring2 observation in the S sector.
- **Belmar/Monmouth Co, NJ** (~45mi, bearing ~175 deg) -- if a cooperative station exists with sufficient data quality.

### 4.5 North Sector Near-Field Gap

The N sector has no station within 50 miles. The closest is Montgomery Orange Co AP at 52.75mi. This is a minor gap -- the NE sector's Westchester (24mi, bearing 35 deg) and NW sector's Teterboro (7mi, bearing 321 deg) provide adjacent near-field coverage that partially compensates.

### 4.6 Geographic Bias Summary

The network exhibits a pronounced **westward and northward bias**:
- W + NW + N + NE sectors: 30 stations (60%)
- E + SE + S + SW sectors: 20 stations (40%)

This bias reflects the asymmetric land/water geography around NYC. To the east and southeast lies the Atlantic Ocean, which physically limits station placement. The bias is therefore an accurate reflection of available observation infrastructure rather than a correctable design choice.

## 5. Meteorological Sector Assessment

### 5.1 WNW Sector (W + NW) -- Cold-Front Detection

**Station count:** 14 (8 W + 6 NW)
**Distance range:** 7.2mi (Teterboro) to 194.9mi (Syracuse)

This sector is responsible for detecting cold fronts approaching from the west and northwest -- the dominant synoptic pattern for temperature drops in NYC. The coverage is **excellent**:
- Near-field (Ring1): 5 stations (Caldwell, Somerset, Aeroflex-Andover, Sussex, Teterboro)
- Regional (Ring2): 5 stations (Mt Pocono, Allentown, Monticello, Wilkes-Barre, plus Mt Pocono straddles the W/WNW boundary)
- Extended (Ring3): 4 stations (Reading, Lancaster, Binghamton, plus overlap)
- Far (Ring4): 2 stations (Selinsgrove, Syracuse)

A cold front approaching from 280 deg would first register at Syracuse/Binghamton (~150-195mi), then Wilkes-Barre/Mt Pocono (~78-99mi), then Aeroflex-Andover/Sussex (~43-45mi), and finally Teterboro/Caldwell (~7-18mi). This provides 4 stages of advance detection over ~8-24 hours -- more than sufficient for day-ahead prediction.

**Verdict:** No improvements needed. This is the network's strongest sector.

### 5.2 SW Sector (S + SW) -- Warm-Front Detection

**Station count:** 10 (3 S + 7 SW)
**Distance range:** 12.4mi (Newark) to 181.4mi (Ocean City)

Warm fronts and southerly warm-air advection events are critical for summer heatwaves and shoulder-season temperature spikes. Assessment:
- Near-field: 1 station (Newark at 12mi, bearing 238 deg -- really more WSW than SSW)
- Regional: 8 stations clustered at 56-100mi (Trenton, Doylestown, NE Phila, S Jersey, Phila, Heritage Field, Atlantic City x2)
- Extended: 0 stations
- Far: 1 station (Ocean City at 181mi)

**Concern:** The SW corridor is well-sampled at 56-100mi but has a gap between 100-150mi (Ring3). A warm front moving NNE from the Chesapeake Bay area would jump from Ocean City (181mi) to the NJ/PA cluster (56-91mi) with no intermediate observation. This is a moderate weakness.

**Potential improvement:** A station near Dover, DE or Salisbury, MD (~120-140mi, bearing ~210-220 deg) would fill the Ring3 gap in this meteorological sector.

### 5.3 Coastal Sector (E + SE) -- Atlantic Moderation

**Station count:** 10 (9 E + 1 SE)
**Distance range:** 4.7mi (LaGuardia) to 135.3mi (Kingston RI)

This sector captures the Atlantic Ocean's moderating influence on NYC temperatures -- the sea breeze effect in summer and maritime warmth in winter. Assessment:
- The E sector is extremely well sampled along Long Island from LaGuardia (5mi) through Montauk (109mi) and into CT/RI (107-135mi).
- The SE sector's sole station (JFK at 14mi) provides only near-field coastal data.
- No stations exist over the open Atlantic, which is an irreducible limitation.

**Key strength:** The Long Island chain (LaGuardia, Farmingdale, Islip, Shirley Brookhaven, Westhampton, Montauk) provides exceptional east-west coastal gradient data. This is more valuable than it might appear -- maritime air masses typically approach from the E/ENE rather than SE due to prevailing wind patterns.

**Verdict:** Adequate for practical purposes. The SE gap cannot be filled due to ocean geography. The E sector's depth partially compensates.

### 5.4 NE Sector (N + NE) -- New England Influence

**Station count:** 16 (8 N + 8 NE)
**Distance range:** 24.0mi (Westchester) to 188.0mi (Boston)

This is the largest meteorological sector. It captures Canadian cold air descending through the Hudson and Connecticut valleys, as well as New England weather systems. Coverage is comprehensive across all rings and both compass sectors. No weaknesses identified.

**Verdict:** No improvements needed.

## 7. Recommendations

### Priority 1 (High Impact): Fill the South Near-Field Gap

**Problem:** No S-sector station within 97 miles.
**Action:** Add a station near Joint Base McGuire-Dix-Lakehurst, NJ (approximate GHCN ID: USW00014780 or USW00093735). This military/FAA station at ~50mi, bearing ~195 deg would provide a crucial mid-range S observation.
**Expected benefit:** Improved detection of warm-air advection from the south, particularly for summer heatwave events and winter thaw episodes.

### Priority 2 (Moderate Impact): Fill the SW Ring3 Gap

**Problem:** The SW meteorological sector jumps from 91mi (Philadelphia) to 181mi (Ocean City) with no Ring3 coverage.
**Action:** Investigate stations near Dover AFB, DE (USW00013707, ~130mi, bearing ~210 deg) or Salisbury, MD (~140mi, bearing ~205 deg).
**Expected benefit:** Better intermediate sampling of warm fronts approaching from the Chesapeake/mid-Atlantic region.

### Priority 3 (Low Impact, Opportunistic): ESE Transition Zone

**Problem:** 36-degree gap between Farmingdale (96 deg) and JFK (132 deg).
**Action:** Search for a GHCN cooperative station on Long Island's south shore near Babylon/Lindenhurst area (~20mi, bearing ~110-120 deg). Availability of quality data is uncertain.
**Expected benefit:** Minor improvement in ESE coastal signal.

### Priority 4 (Informational): Accept the SSE Ocean Gap

**Problem:** 62-degree gap from 132-194 degrees is over the Atlantic Ocean.
**Action:** No action possible. This gap is an irreducible geographic constraint. Document it and accept that the model must infer SSE influence from adjacent E and S sector stations.

### General Observations

1. **The network is well-designed overall.** With 50 stations, the coverage of land-based directions is thorough. The W/NW upstream sector (14 stations) is appropriately the densest, reflecting the dominant direction of weather system approach.

2. **Coverage imbalance is geography-driven.** The 60/40 split favoring western/northern sectors accurately reflects the asymmetric land mass around NYC. Attempting to equalize sector counts would require non-existent ocean stations.

3. **Ring2 is the workhorse.** The 50-100mi ring has 20 stations (40% of the network), which is appropriate since this distance range typically provides the strongest day-ahead predictive signal.

4. **Diminishing returns are likely.** With the current 5-year dataset (1,277 training samples), Phase 4 experiments already showed that more than ~14 stations causes overfitting. The current 50-station network is designed for the Phase 6 scale-up (25 years). Adding 1-2 more stations to fill gaps is worthwhile only after the data volume increases.

5. **For the current model's purposes, the SE/SSE gap is the only material weakness**, and it cannot be practically resolved. The model should be evaluated specifically on days with onshore SSE flow to quantify the actual prediction penalty, if any.

## 8. Implementation Status

**Date:** 2026-02-08

### Priority 1 — S Near-Field Gap: IMPLEMENTED
A station near Joint Base McGuire-Dix-Lakehurst, NJ has been added to `config_expanded.py`. This provides Ring1/Ring2 S-sector coverage at ~50mi, bearing ~195 deg, closing the 97-mile near-field void in the south.

### Priority 2 — SW Ring3 Gap: IMPLEMENTED
A station near Dover AFB, DE has been added to `config_expanded.py`. This provides Ring3 coverage at ~130mi, bearing ~210 deg, bridging the gap between Philadelphia (91mi) and Ocean City (181mi) in the SW meteorological corridor.

### Priority 3 — ESE Transition Zone: CONFIRMED UNFILLABLE
Exhaustive search of the GHCN station inventory (ghcnd-stations.txt and ghcnd-inventory.txt) confirmed that no suitable station exists to fill the 36-degree gap between Farmingdale (96 deg) and JFK (132 deg). Key findings:
- **35 GHCN stations** exist in the target geographic box (lat 40.55-40.75, lon -73.65 to -73.25).
- **Only 1 USW airport station** was found: Mitchell Field (USW00014708, 19.6mi, 99.1 deg) — a decommissioned military airfield with TMAX data only from 1949-1961.
- **Best cooperative stations** all have discontinued TMAX records:
  - Mineola (USC00305377): 18.6mi, 99.7 deg — TMAX 1938-2011 (stopped 2011)
  - Wantagh Cedar Creek (USC00308946): 25.8mi, 109.3 deg — TMAX 1976-2011 (stopped 2011)
  - Oceanside (USC00306138): 20.6mi, 119.3 deg — TMAX 1994-2010 (stopped 2010)
  - Babylon (USC00300351): 31.8mi, 97.6 deg — TMAX 1918-1959 (stopped 1959)
- **Remaining stations** are US1* personal/CoCoRaHS stations with no TMAX data.
- This confirms the original assessment: most Long Island south shore reporting stations are cooperative network sites rather than first-order airports, and none have current TMAX coverage meeting the 1985-2024 requirement.

### Priority 4 — SSE Ocean Gap: ACCEPTED
No action taken. The 62-degree gap (132-194 deg) over the Atlantic Ocean remains an irreducible geographic constraint, as documented.

### Summary
The station network has been expanded from 50 to ~52 surrounding stations. Two of the three actionable gaps have been filled. The remaining ESE gap is a data availability limitation, not a network design flaw.
